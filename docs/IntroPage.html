Introduction to RDT

Section Author - James Mueller

The basic definition of relational dialectics has a few components. First, that the words and phrases we use inherently carry meaning, and that second, the meaning applied to those words by people may be different from person to person. Furthermore, when two parties have a conversation, the words and phrases are not said in a random order, but instead we might first exchange a greeting before talking about how we feel, or something going on in your life. Also, there isn't necessarily a strict structure that conversations must follow, there is a flow that can be traced from response to repsonse. RDT states that it is important to examine each individual response in the context of that conversation flow.

Dr. Leslie Baxter, in their paper Relational DialecticsTheory listed these three things (words have meaning, people apply differnt meanings to words, responses occur within the context of the flow of a conversation) as the first three primary tenets of RDT. In that same paper, Baxter further states that " Fourth, discourses are rarely on equal footing meaning systems typically have unequal power in the meaning-making process. Prior meanings function as semantic precedents for current and future meaning-making. Thus, meaning-making is less a semantic democracy and more a struggle among semantic unequals: the centered, often taken-for-granted meaning system inherited from the utterance chain and alternative, more marginalized meaning systems" (Baxter).


Baxter further goes on to say "Although individual persons, groups, or institutions might invoke particular discourses, it is the power held by dominant discourses that affords greater voice-influence and understandingâ€”to some individuals and groups over others who voice less dominant, muted discourses" (Baxter). Basically what Dr. Baxter is explaining is that in any given dicourse, it is rare that any two parties involved in the discourse will have equivalent "power", and thus certain individuals will have a larger pull on the direction of the flow of the conversation.
